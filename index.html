Global Trading of Wild and Captive Animals in 2016

Importation

The importation of captive and wild animals globally, showing the percentage of wild animals imported per country. The darker the country, the more wild animal imports and lower captive animals imports, and vice versa for lighter countries.

Majority of countries import a relatively equal amount of wild and captive animals. Africa shows more countries importing captive animals comapred to wild.

More developed regions tend show higher importation rates of wild animals such as North America, Europe and Australia which may be more expensive to import compared to captive animals.

Exportation

The exporting of captive and wild animals globally. The darker the country, the more wild animal imports and lower captive animals imports, and vice versa for lighter countries.

Majority of Europe exports more captive animals than wild animals. Most of North and South America export mainly wild animals.

A larger portion of African countries had no export records, indicating that either they do not export wild or captive animals, or the records were not registered with the CITES organisation.

Animal Classification

The network of animal classification heirarchy, in order of animal kingdom, order, class, family, genus, and species from the inside to outside of the sunburst chart. The darker the classification, the more wild animal imports and lower captive animals imports, and vice versa for classifications that are lighter.

The Aves class (birds) and all orders, genuses, families, and species that stem from it are mainly captive animals whereas the Mammalia class consists of 90% of wild animals.

Whereas the Reptilia class has the largest number of records, with the Sauria order being 85% traded from the wild but the Serpentes order consisted of 42% of wild animals being traded. Interestingly, records of python bivittatus species showed only 8% were wild animals, the large majority being captive.

Protection

The protection level for animals traded, where the darker green represents wild animals and the lighter green represents captive animals traded. Protection level is defined as trade is either detrimental to the species or beneficial, the latter being used as a control mechanism.

The proportion of wild and captive species is quite even between both protection levels, and wild beneficial species being traded more often compared to captive.

There was an overwhlemingly larger portion of detrimental species being traded compared to beneficial species, perhaps they are more wanted species.

Purpose

The purpose for exporting or importing the animal varies between commerical and personal reasons. The darker green represents wild animals and the lighter green represents captive animals traded.

A large majority of the purpose for trading was commercial, with a total of 13,883 records, with roughly equal captive and wild animals, 49% and 51% respectively.

Majority of records for medical and zoo purposes were captive animals. Whereas trading animals for educational, hunting trophies and scientific purposes mainly consisted of wild animals.

Condition

The condition the animal was traded in, usually involving what part of the animal was traded. The darker green represents wild animals and the lighter green represents captive animals traded.

The condition or part of animal that was traded varies greatly. The largest condition traded was live animals, with 8,236 records, followed by small leather products with 4,280 records. For live animals, 68% were captive animals whereas for small leather products, only 35% were captive.

Although it appears there are more wild animal records, there are just more conditions that only consisted of a small number of wild animals, such as horns and fur products.

By Maddie Savic. Information from CITES Wildlife Trade 2016
